By mass, oxygen is the third most abundant element in the universe, after hydrogen and helium. At standard temperature and pressure, two atoms of the element bind to form dioxygen, a colorless and odorless diatomic gas with the formula O2.
The bald eagle is a bird of prey found in North America. A sea eagle, it has two known subspecies and forms a species pair with the white-tailed eagle.
Its range includes most of Canada and Alaska, all of the contiguous United States, and northern Mexico. It is found near large open water with an abundant food supply and old-growth trees for nesting.
Photosynthesis is a process used by plants and other organisms to convert light energy into chemical energy that, through cellular respiration, can later be released to fuel the organism's activities.
This chemical energy is stored in carbohydrate molecules, such as sugars and starches, which are synthesized from carbon dioxide and water.
The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications made of stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and other materials, generally built along an east-to-west line across the historical northern borders of China.
The wall was built to protect the Chinese states and empires against the raids and invasions of the various nomadic groups of the Eurasian Steppe.
Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that provides a description of the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles.
It is the foundation of all quantum physics including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.
The Amazon rainforest, alternatively the Amazon jungle, also known in English as Amazonia, is a moist broadleaf tropical rainforest in the Amazon biome that covers most of the Amazon basin of South America.
This basin encompasses 7,000,000 km2, of which 5,500,000 km2 are covered by the rainforest. This region includes territory belonging to nine nations and 3,344 formally acknowledged indigenous territories.
Artificial intelligence, commonly referred to as AI, is intelligence demonstrated by machines, as opposed to the natural intelligence displayed by animals including humans.
Leading AI textbooks define the field as the study of intelligent agents: any device that perceives its environment and takes actions that maximize its chance of achieving its goals.
The human brain is the central organ of the human nervous system, and with the spinal cord makes up the central nervous system. The brain consists of the cerebrum, the brainstem and the cerebellum.
It controls most of the activities of the body, processing, integrating, and coordinating the information it receives from the sense organs, and making decisions as to the instructions sent to the rest of the body.
The Internet is the global system of interconnected computer networks that uses the Internet protocol suite to communicate between networks and devices.
It is a network of networks that consists of private, public, academic, business, and government networks of local to global scope, linked by a broad array of electronic, wireless, and optical networking technologies.
The periodic table, also known as the periodic table of elements, is a tabular display of the chemical elements, which are arranged by atomic number, electron configuration, and recurring chemical properties.
The structure of the table shows periodic trends. The seven rows of the table, called periods, generally have metals on the left and nonmetals on the right.
Machine learning is a method of data analysis that automates analytical model building. It is based on the idea that systems can learn from data, identify patterns and make decisions with minimal human intervention.
As a branch of artificial intelligence, it is a method of data analysis that automates analytical model building. Because of new computing technologies, machine learning today is not like machine learning of the past.
The speed of light in vacuum, commonly denoted c, is a universal physical constant important in physics. Its exact value is defined as 299,792,458 metres per second.
It is exact because, by international agreement, a metre is defined as the length of the path travelled by light in vacuum during a time interval of 1/299,792,458 of a second.
DNA, or deoxyribonucleic acid, is a molecule composed of two polynucleotide chains that coil around each other to form a double helix carrying genetic instructions for the development, functioning, growth and reproduction of all known organisms and many viruses.
DNA and ribonucleic acid are nucleic acids. Alongside proteins, lipids and complex carbohydrates, nucleic acids are one of the four major types of macromolecules that are essential for all known forms of life.
The solar system consists of the Sun and everything that orbits around it, including the eight planets, their moons, the asteroid belt, comets, and other objects.
It formed 4.6 billion years ago from the gravitational collapse of a giant molecular cloud. The vast majority of the solar system's mass is in the Sun, with the majority of the remaining mass contained in the planet Jupiter.
Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. These shifts may be natural, such as through variations in the solar cycle.
But since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to burning fossil fuels like coal, oil and gas.
The Renaissance was a period in European history marking the transition from the Middle Ages to modernity and covering the 15th and 16th centuries.
In addition to the standard periodization, proponents of a "long Renaissance" may include the 14th-century developments in Italy. The Renaissance was characterized by an effort to revive and surpass ideas and achievements of classical antiquity.
Gravity, or gravitation, is a natural phenomenon by which all things with mass or energy—including planets, stars, galaxies, and even light—are attracted to one another.
On Earth, gravity gives weight to physical objects, and the Moon's gravity causes the ocean tides. The gravitational attraction of the original gaseous matter present in the Universe caused it to begin coalescing and forming stars.
The electric car, also known as a battery electric vehicle or BEV, uses one or more electric motors or traction motors for propulsion. It uses chemical energy stored in rechargeable battery packs.
The first practical electric vehicles appeared in the 1880s, but with the advent of internal combustion engines, they all but disappeared from use.
Neural networks, also known as artificial neural networks or simulated neural networks, are a subset of machine learning and are at the heart of deep learning algorithms.
Their name and structure are inspired by the human brain, mimicking the way that biological neurons signal to one another.
The theory of evolution by natural selection was first formulated in Charles Darwin's book On the Origin of Species in 1859. Darwin defined evolution as descent with modification, the idea that species change over time, give rise to new species, and share a common ancestor.
The mechanism that Darwin proposed for evolution is natural selection. Because resources are limited in nature, organisms with heritable traits that favor survival and reproduction will tend to leave more offspring than their peers.
Blockchain is a system of recording information in a way that makes it difficult or impossible to change, hack, or cheat the system. A blockchain is essentially a digital ledger of transactions that is duplicated and distributed across the entire network of computer systems on the blockchain.
Each block in the chain contains a number of transactions, and every time a new transaction occurs on the blockchain, a record of that transaction is added to every participant's ledger.
The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite. At about one-quarter the diameter of Earth, it is the fifth largest satellite in the Solar System and the largest and most massive relative to its parent planet.
The Moon is a planetary-mass object with a differentiated rocky body, making it a satellite planet under the geophysical definitions of the term. It lacks any significant atmosphere, hydrosphere, or magnetic field.
Black holes are points in space that are so dense that even light cannot escape their gravity. A black hole is formed when a star with at least three times the mass of the sun collapses at the end of its life.
The boundary of the black hole is called the event horizon. Anything that crosses the event horizon is inevitably pulled into the black hole and cannot escape.
The Python programming language was created by Guido van Rossum and first released in 1991. Python's design philosophy emphasizes code readability and the use of significant indentation.
Python is dynamically typed and garbage-collected. It supports multiple programming paradigms, including structured, object-oriented and functional programming.
Water covers 71 percent of the Earth's surface. It is vital for all known forms of life. On Earth, 96.5 percent of the planet's water is found in the global oceans, 1.7 percent in the groundwater, 1.7 percent in the glaciers and ice caps of Antarctica and Greenland.
Only 2.5 percent of this water is freshwater, and 98.8 percent of that water is in ice and groundwater. Less than 0.3 percent of all freshwater is in rivers, lakes, and the atmosphere.
The Large Hadron Collider is the world's largest and highest-energy particle collider. It was built by the European Organization for Nuclear Research between 1998 and 2008 in collaboration with over 10,000 scientists and hundreds of universities and laboratories.
The collider lies in a tunnel 27 kilometres in circumference and as deep as 175 metres beneath the Franco-Swiss border near Geneva, Switzerland.
Photons are elementary particles that are the quantum of the electromagnetic field, including electromagnetic radiation such as light and radio waves, and the force carrier for the electromagnetic force.
Photons are massless, so they always move at the speed of light in vacuum. Like all elementary particles, photons are currently best explained by quantum mechanics and exhibit wave–particle duality.
The Hubble Space Telescope is a space telescope that was launched into low Earth orbit in 1990 and remains in operation. It was not the first space telescope, but it is one of the largest and most versatile.
The telescope is named after astronomer Edwin Hubble, who was key in confirming that the Milky Way is just one of many galaxies in the universe.
Semiconductor devices are electronic components that exploit the electronic properties of semiconductor materials, principally silicon, germanium, and gallium arsenide.
Semiconductor devices have replaced vacuum tubes in most applications and are used in all modern electronics. They can be used as conductors or insulators, changing their role by applying voltages.
Cryptography is the practice and study of techniques for securing communication in the presence of third parties called adversaries. Cryptography is about constructing and analyzing protocols that prevent third parties or the public from reading private messages.
Modern cryptography is heavily based on mathematical theory and computer science practice. Cryptographic algorithms are designed around computational hardness assumptions, making such algorithms hard to break in actual practice by any adversary.
The James Webb Space Telescope is a space telescope designed to conduct infrared astronomy. Its high infrared resolution and sensitivity allow it to view objects too old, distant, or faint for the Hubble Space Telescope.
This is expected to enable a broad range of investigations across the fields of astronomy and cosmology, such as observation of the first stars and the formation of the first galaxies.
Plate tectonics is a scientific theory describing the large-scale motion of the plates making up Earth's lithosphere since tectonic plates were formed between 3.3 and 3.5 billion years ago.
The model builds on the concept of continental drift, an idea developed during the first decades of the 20th century. Tectonic plates are composed of oceanic lithosphere and the thicker continental lithosphere, each topped by its own kind of crust.
The human immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, organs, and the substances they make that helps the body fight infections and other diseases.
The innate immune system is the body's initial defense against pathogens, providing a rapid but nonspecific response. The adaptive immune system, in contrast, offers a targeted attack against specific pathogens.
Vaccination is the administration of a vaccine to help the immune system develop protection from a disease. Vaccines contain a microorganism or virus in a weakened, live or killed state, or proteins or toxins from the organism.
In stimulating the body's adaptive immunity, vaccines help prevent sickness from an infectious disease. When a vaccinated person encounters that exact microbe later in life, their immune system is ready for it.
The Milky Way is the galaxy that includes our Solar System, with the name describing the galaxy's appearance from Earth. It is a barred spiral galaxy with an estimated visible diameter of 100,000 light-years, and the thickness is estimated to be between 1,000 and 4,000 light-years.
The Milky Way has several satellite galaxies and is part of the Local Group of galaxies, which form part of the Virgo Supercluster, which is itself a component of the Laniakea Supercluster.
The carbon cycle is the biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged among the biosphere, pedosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere of the Earth.
Carbon is the main component of biological compounds as well as a major component of many minerals such as limestone. Along with the nitrogen cycle and the water cycle, the carbon cycle comprises a sequence of events that are key to make Earth capable of sustaining life.
Superconductivity is a set of physical properties observed in certain materials where electrical resistance vanishes and magnetic flux fields are expelled from the material.
Any material exhibiting these properties is a superconductor. Unlike an ordinary metallic conductor, whose resistance decreases gradually as its temperature is lowered even down to near absolute zero.
Nuclear fusion is a reaction in which two or more atomic nuclei are combined to form one or more different atomic nuclei and subatomic particles. The difference in mass between the reactants and products is manifested as either the release or the absorption of energy.
This difference in mass arises due to the difference in nuclear binding energy between the atomic nuclei before and after the reaction.
The theory of relativity usually encompasses two interrelated theories by Albert Einstein: special relativity and general relativity. Special relativity applies to all physical phenomena in the absence of gravity.
General relativity explains the law of gravitation and its relation to the forces of nature. It applies to the cosmological and astrophysical realm, including astronomy.
Language models are statistical tools that learn to predict the probability of sequences of words. Modern large language models are based on transformer architectures, a type of neural network that uses self-attention mechanisms to process sequences of tokens.
These models are pre-trained on massive text corpora and can be fine-tuned for specific tasks. The scale of training data and model parameters has a significant impact on the capabilities of language models.
Edge computing is a distributed computing paradigm which brings computation and data storage closer to the sources of data. This is expected to improve response times and save bandwidth.
Edge computing pushes the frontier of computing applications, data, and services away from centralized nodes to the logical extremes of a network. The computation is done near the edge of the network, near the source of the data.
Convolutional neural networks are a type of deep learning algorithm that are particularly effective for analyzing visual data. They use convolutional layers to automatically learn spatial hierarchies of features from input images.
CNNs have revolutionized computer vision tasks such as image classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation. They are inspired by the visual cortex of animals.
The field of natural language processing combines linguistics and machine learning to enable computers to understand and generate human language. Key tasks include text classification, sentiment analysis, machine translation, and question answering.
Transformers have become the dominant architecture for NLP tasks since their introduction in 2017, achieving state-of-the-art results across virtually all language benchmarks.
Reinforcement learning is a type of machine learning where an agent learns to make decisions by interacting with an environment to maximize cumulative reward. It is used in robotics, game playing, and autonomous systems.
Key algorithms include Q-learning, policy gradients, and actor-critic methods. Deep reinforcement learning combines neural networks with reinforcement learning, enabling agents to learn from raw sensory input.
The GPU, or graphics processing unit, was originally designed to accelerate the creation of images in a frame buffer intended for output to a display device. Since the late 2000s, GPUs have been increasingly used for non-graphic applications.
The highly parallel structure of GPUs makes them more efficient than general-purpose CPUs for algorithms that process large blocks of data in parallel, such as matrix multiplication in deep learning.
Attention mechanisms are a part of neural network architectures that allow the model to focus on different parts of the input when producing an output. They were introduced in the context of machine translation to help with long sequences.
The scaled dot-product attention computes compatibility between queries and keys to weight the values. Multi-head attention applies multiple attention operations in parallel, allowing the model to attend to information from different representation subspaces.
Fine-tuning is the process of taking a pre-trained neural network model and adapting it to a new task. The model is initialized with weights from pre-training and then trained further on task-specific data.
This approach is more sample-efficient than training from scratch and typically requires far less data. Fine-tuning is the standard approach for applying large language models to downstream tasks.
Transformers were introduced in the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" by Vaswani et al. Unlike previous sequence-to-sequence models based on recurrent neural networks, transformers rely entirely on attention mechanisms to draw global dependencies between input and output.
The transformer architecture has become the foundation for state-of-the-art models across natural language processing, computer vision, and audio processing. BERT, GPT, and T5 are all based on transformers.
Distributed training refers to the techniques used to train machine learning models across multiple computing devices, whether multiple GPUs on the same machine or GPUs distributed across multiple machines.
Model parallelism splits the model layers across different devices. Data parallelism replicates the full model on each device and splits the training data. Pipeline parallelism partitions the model by layers into stages processed in sequence.
Tokenization is the process of converting text into smaller units called tokens, which are the basic units used by language models. Tokens can be words, subwords, or characters depending on the tokenization scheme.
Modern language models typically use byte-pair encoding or SentencePiece tokenization, which efficiently represent common words as single tokens while splitting rare words into subword units.
Precision in machine learning refers to the numerical format used to represent model weights and activations. Common formats include FP32, FP16, BF16, INT8, and INT4.
Lower precision reduces memory usage and increases computation speed but can introduce quantization error. The choice of precision involves a tradeoff between model size, speed, and accuracy.
Inference optimization refers to techniques to make neural network inference faster and more memory-efficient without retraining the model. Techniques include quantization, pruning, knowledge distillation, operator fusion, and batch size optimization.
These techniques are essential for deploying large models on resource-constrained hardware such as mobile devices, embedded systems, and edge computing platforms.
Embedding layers are components of neural networks that map discrete inputs, such as words or tokens, to continuous vector representations called embeddings. These vectors capture semantic relationships between inputs.
Word embeddings like Word2Vec and GloVe were among the first successful approaches to learning distributed representations of words. Modern language models learn contextual embeddings that capture the meaning of words in context.
Batch normalization is a technique to improve the training of deep neural networks by normalizing the inputs to each layer. It reduces the internal covariate shift problem and acts as a regularizer.
It was introduced by Ioffe and Szegedy in 2015 and has become a standard component of modern deep learning architectures. During training, it normalizes activations using the mean and variance of the current batch.
Transfer learning is a machine learning technique where a model trained on one task is reused as the starting point for a model on a different but related task. It leverages knowledge gained from previous experience.
This approach is particularly powerful in deep learning, where large pre-trained models can be fine-tuned on small datasets to achieve strong performance on specific tasks with limited labeled data.
The concept of overfitting refers to a model that learns the training data too well, including the noise and outliers, and thus performs poorly on new, unseen data. It is the opposite of underfitting.
Regularization techniques such as dropout, weight decay, and early stopping are used to prevent overfitting. Cross-validation helps detect overfitting by evaluating model performance on held-out data.
Recurrent neural networks are a class of artificial neural networks where connections between nodes form a directed graph along a temporal sequence. This allows them to exhibit temporal dynamic behavior.
LSTMs and GRUs are variants of RNNs designed to address the vanishing gradient problem, enabling them to learn long-range dependencies. Despite their effectiveness, RNNs have been largely supplanted by transformers for sequence modeling.
Generative adversarial networks consist of two neural networks trained simultaneously: a generator that creates fake data and a discriminator that tries to distinguish fake from real data.
The generator improves by learning to fool the discriminator, while the discriminator improves by becoming better at detecting fakes. This adversarial training process leads to the generator producing increasingly realistic outputs.
The perceptron is the simplest form of a neural network, consisting of a single layer of input nodes connected to an output node through weighted connections. It is a binary classifier that maps real-valued inputs to a single binary output value.
The perceptron learning algorithm adjusts the weights based on prediction errors. Although limited to linearly separable problems, it laid the foundation for more complex multilayer neural networks.
Convolutional neural networks learn hierarchical representations of data through convolution operations. The early layers learn simple features like edges and colors, while deeper layers learn complex features like object parts and textures.
Pooling layers reduce spatial dimensions, reducing computational cost and providing translation invariance. Fully connected layers at the end of the network perform the final classification or regression.
Model compression encompasses techniques to create smaller, faster models from larger ones. The main approaches are quantization, pruning, knowledge distillation, and low-rank factorization.
The goal is to reduce the computational and memory requirements while maintaining acceptable model quality. Compressed models are essential for deployment on edge devices with limited resources.
The perplexity metric measures how well a probability model predicts a sample. For language models, lower perplexity means the model assigns higher probability to the test data.
Perplexity is computed as the exponential of the average cross-entropy loss. It is commonly used to compare different language models on the same dataset, with the WikiText-2 dataset being a standard benchmark.
Gradient descent is an optimization algorithm used to minimize the loss function of a neural network by iteratively adjusting the model parameters. It computes the gradient of the loss with respect to each parameter.
Stochastic gradient descent uses a randomly selected subset of the training data called a mini-batch to compute the gradient. Adaptive optimizers such as Adam and AdaGrad adjust the learning rate for each parameter automatically.
Knowledge distillation is a model compression technique in which a smaller student model is trained to reproduce the behavior of a larger teacher model. The student learns from the teacher's soft output probabilities.
Soft targets contain more information than hard labels because they capture the relative similarities between classes as perceived by the teacher model. This enables the student to learn a more generalizable representation.
Sparse attention mechanisms reduce the computational complexity of attention from quadratic to linear in sequence length by restricting which tokens can attend to each other.
Methods include local windowed attention, global attention on special tokens, and learned sparsity patterns. Sparse transformers can process much longer sequences than standard transformers.
Mixed precision training uses a combination of half-precision and single-precision floating-point formats. The forward and backward passes are computed in FP16 for speed, while weights are maintained in FP32 for numerical stability.
Loss scaling is used to prevent gradient underflow in FP16. Mixed precision training can provide a 2-3x speedup and reduce memory usage by up to 50 percent compared to full precision training.
The ONNX Runtime is a cross-platform, high performance ML inferencing and training accelerator. It supports multiple hardware targets including CPUs, GPUs, FPGAs, and dedicated AI accelerators.
ONNX models can be exported from PyTorch, TensorFlow, and other frameworks and run efficiently with ONNX Runtime. It applies graph-level optimizations and operator fusion to maximize hardware utilization.
Semantic segmentation is the task of assigning a class label to every pixel in an image. It is a form of dense prediction that provides a fine-grained understanding of the visual scene.
Applications include autonomous driving, medical image analysis, and satellite imagery interpretation. Modern semantic segmentation models use encoder-decoder architectures with skip connections.
Embedding dimension is the size of the vector representation used for tokens in a language model. Larger embedding dimensions allow the model to capture more information but increase computational cost.
The relationship between embedding size, number of attention heads, and feed-forward size is carefully designed in transformer architectures to balance capacity and efficiency.
Pruning is a model compression technique that removes weights or entire neurons from a neural network that contribute little to its output. Structured pruning removes entire filters or attention heads.
Unstructured pruning removes individual weights, resulting in sparse matrices. Pruned models can achieve significant size reduction with minimal quality loss when combined with fine-tuning after pruning.
Speculative decoding is an inference acceleration technique where a small draft model generates candidate tokens which are then verified in parallel by the large target model.
When the draft model's predictions match the target model's distribution, multiple tokens can be accepted per forward pass of the large model. This can provide a 2-4x speedup over standard autoregressive decoding.
The vocabulary size of a language model refers to the number of unique tokens in the model's tokenizer. Larger vocabularies can represent text more efficiently but increase the size of the embedding layers.
Most modern large language models have vocabularies in the range of 32,000 to 200,000 tokens. The choice of vocabulary size involves tradeoffs between representation efficiency and model size.
Mixture of experts is a neural network architecture where different parts of the network, called experts, are specialized for different inputs. A gating network selects which experts to use for each input.
In sparse mixture of experts models, only a subset of experts are activated for each token, reducing the computational cost while maintaining model capacity. This is used in models like GPT-4 and Mixtral.
The Llama model family, developed by Meta AI, represents a series of open foundation language models trained on publicly available data. Llama 3.1 was released with 8B, 70B, and 405B parameter variants.
These models match or exceed proprietary models on many benchmarks and have enabled a thriving open-source AI ecosystem. The release of Llama sparked widespread adoption of local LLM inference.
Flash Attention is an IO-aware attention algorithm that computes attention in a memory-efficient manner by minimizing reads and writes to HBM, the high-bandwidth memory of GPUs.
Standard attention algorithms materialize the large attention matrix in HBM, which is slow and memory-intensive for long sequences. Flash Attention computes attention in tiles that fit in the much faster SRAM.
Quantization-aware training incorporates quantization operations into the training process, allowing the model to adapt its weights to the constraints of the target numerical precision.
This typically produces better results than post-training quantization, especially at very low bit widths like INT4 or INT2, because the model learns to compensate for quantization error during training.
Continuous batching, also known as iteration-level scheduling, dynamically adds and removes sequences from the running batch during inference. This improves GPU utilization compared to static batching.
Modern LLM serving frameworks like vLLM and TGI use continuous batching to achieve high throughput while maintaining low latency. This is essential for production deployment of large language models.
The KV cache stores the key and value matrices from previous tokens during autoregressive generation, avoiding redundant computation. Memory for the KV cache grows linearly with sequence length and batch size.
Efficient KV cache management is critical for serving long-context models. Paged attention, used in vLLM, manages the KV cache like virtual memory, enabling large batches with variable sequence lengths.
CUDA is a parallel computing platform and programming model developed by NVIDIA for general-purpose processing on GPUs. It enables dramatic increases in computing performance by harnessing the power of the GPU.
CUDA is used extensively for deep learning workloads. The CUDA toolkit includes a compiler, libraries like cuBLAS and cuDNN, and profiling tools. Different GPU architectures support different CUDA compute capabilities.
The sm_120 compute capability refers to the NVIDIA Blackwell architecture, introduced with the RTX 5090 and H200 data center GPUs. It introduces new data types and capabilities for AI inference workloads.
Standard PyTorch releases do not include support for sm_120, requiring users with RTX 5090 GPUs to install a nightly build compiled with CUDA 13.0 or later to access GPU acceleration.
